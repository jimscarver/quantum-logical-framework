I never set out to build a new way of seeing the universe. I was just a kid who liked asking “why?” — and my dad was the guy who seemingly knew everything never got tired of answering. I was in my twenties when he finnally answered my question "I don't know.". He read everything but his passion was science.

### The Knee Lessons

I learned quantum physics and general relativity on my father’s knee. Literally.

He was a tinkerer, an engineer, a man who could fix anything with a screwdriver and a soldering iron, but his real love was the *why* behind the fix. His knowledge extended to the fringes of science. 

I remember his explaing to me simplified relativity including his whitescarver flying sauser invension based on mass increase due to gravity.   He wrote letters to scientists includint Einstein without response until I was a teen ager. But to me the flying sausor was proven and I was sad when my father set my down to explain why it wouldn't work. However this was an AHA moment learning that what they teach about relativity is wrong. People still think that we see relatively is real but locally nothing changes. Mass change due to motion is a usefull illution, locally nothing changes, motion is only relative, even falling into a black hole. Later my father cautioned me, "Read Einstien, don't read about Einstein." And it seemed all accounts of Einstien were misleading at best. I felt like I had a personal relationship with Einstein and had dialogs with him in my dreams. They say he didn't understand quantum physics, but his record proves he understood it better than anyone. In the end, my deterministic model vindicates him.

Dad taught the only thing happening in this world was quantum mechanical events manifesting electron interaction keeping us from falling through our chair. He taught me everthing was equal and opposite action. I learned later that this requires zero free action (ZFA). The universe can't get free action from anywhere.

### School Days and the Family Business

I did not excel in mathimatics until 8th grade. I was always bad at numbers but excelled in algebra and geometry. My real education still happened at home. My fater and I solved sillogisms and discussed Froyd and Young etc. in addition to physics and engineering. He challanged me to build a crystal radio.  While the teacher talked about “real numbers” I protested that numbers that were produced by no algorithum were not real. In my senior year learning calculus I protested that limits going to infinity were impossible and did my own proof of the fundimental theorm of calculus using difference tables. The head of the department verified my proof and I got a merit award in mathematics which my mother cherished to the day she died.

After school I studied compuer programming. Summers I worked at the family business **Whitescarver Transportation** — the trucking company that hauled freight for Grand Union supermarkets out of Carlstadt, New Jersey. Trucks, loading docks, diesel smells, and long nights watching my dad and uncles keep those big rigs rolling on time. I worked on the loading dock and repair shop until 3:30 and played with the computers until 6 when my father drove us home. Later I worked in the office as a programmer and develped  warehouse scheduling and vehical routing the dispachers loved. IBM education was free for IBM customers at the time so I took every course I could, learned a variety of programming languages and operations research. 

### Widener College

After high school I headed to **Widener College** (now Widener University). I majored in electrical engineering because I already knew everything about computer science (ha).  I had fun with analog computers solving differential equations almost magically. The professor proved to us that the most data you can send over a phone line was 300 bits per second. He thought I just didn't get it. Collage seemed a waste of time since they were onlt teaching half truths so in 1968 I quit school and joined the hippy revolution, hitched to calafornia and then lived in iglues and under bridges and treked the mountains around Aspen colorado. I did drugs and experienced the cosmic consiousness of the information universe.

### Marriage and the Next Chapter

After a year I ventured home on bicicle through wyomming and nebraska, then hitched back and stopped at Widner where I met my wife to be with whom we had 5 children. When I got home my mother demanded that I stay, so I went back to work as a programmer for the family business. Eventually I was tired writing the same programs as thousands of other programmers writing payroll, accounts payable, accounting etc. Should't computers eliminate this redundancy? perhaps AI is the answer. I wrote an eliza like program that passed the turing test trivially by including specific information about people in the office. In 1970 I decided to go back to school to learn AI.  

### NJIT, EIES, Murray Turoff & Star Roxanne Hiltz

Then came **NJIT** — New Jersey Institute of Technology. This time I magored in computer science and minored in EE. That was where things got electric in a whole new ways.

I landed in the world of computer-mediated communication, working with the pioneers themselves: **Murray Turoff**, the visionary who developed the first chat system and **Roxanne Hiltz**, a true social scientist who taught me scientific analysis. Murray said to me "You can spend your whole live building one computer as smart as a human, or develope a system that enables the collective intelligence amoung humans. That became my new mission. We built **EIES** — the Electronic Information Exchange System — one of the very first online conferencing systems in the world. We were literally inventing how humans talk to each other through machines before the internet. At the time the chairman of the computer science department remarked, "nobody will ever waste the power of a computer doing electronic mail. WE were far ahead of our time.  We became a sort of true internet, connecting mailnet, bitnet, arpanet and the commerial packet switch networks introducing the internet to business. We develops SGML markup to tailor the user interface years before the world wide web and introduced forms, making the web interactive and objects (XML) to the world wide web community. OPur research results have largely been forgotten and the results corroborated only in this century largely without crediting our work.

I developed the idea of an interface language, called Interact, allowing second order user programming. Groups developed groupware for their particular communication needs on top of EIES using Interact. Thousants of commands were developed largley by users.

Initially my computer experience made me valuable as a research assistant. I moved
Murray into his office an set up his teletype terminal. I participated in the EIES design and when funding came in I was hired as a programmer and continued school part time. It was an exciting time as the new graduate school of computer science was forming. Everyone was involved in everyones research.

I often had lunch with a group of physics professors arguing fruitlessly that black holes were huge inside because time became space and there could be no singularity inside becaus the gravity in the center of a mass is necessarily zero. And that gravity is local and quantum as if spacetime was falling into masses bending space. I tortured my physics professor in understanding electron interaction. We would get an answer but got no clue how or why. Gradually I learned that each square root of minus one folded into a new dimension but the folding was clearly half spin, not the spin one a photon was supposed to be. Finally he gave me the answer. The photon was indeed half spin, but the antiphoton going back in time (the history) was also half spin, adding to spin one.

In mathematics, diferencial equations, linear algebra, and graph theory, I got out of doing the homework by solving the extra credit problems. I was the only one. I think other students recented me.

My AI interest never faded. I became an expert in string languages and regular expressions. My senior progect was a deductive prover meta language employing second order logic.

When I became director of EIES2 things became political. I am no good at politics and grant awards recieved for proposals I significantly contributed to got spent largely by other faculty members. I had insufficient funding to meet my ojective and my work ended up satifying the deliverables for other faculty whos projects failed.  Eventually I had no funding and left NJIT. Independently I had continued the quest for collective intelligence  developing the communty WikiWorld which later became FreeTrust, Dividao, CoLab, and joined Rchain and became a director of the  Digital live collective. All have faded into oblivion. It turns out I have not been an effective collaborator. But I have not given up.

### Consulting Years

After NJIT I went into consulting with NJIT cohorts. Big companies, small startups, government projects — wherever someone needed systems that actually worked. Everyone was jumping on the world wide web but  could only make pretty pages and not make it do anything worthwhile. We developed the first complex travel fulfullment, the first online banking system system that fullfilled accounts and loans totally online and converted documents for the likes of AT&T and the Catolic Church to the web with automatic links and glosseries. Our web expertise made us very valuable until the dot com crash. I turned to process automation and financial servises, online trading and developed arbitrage products. We won a DOD cyber security research grant on determinine risk based on maximum relative entropy. Finally back to my roots in transportation problems until I finaly landed my dream job.

### Shannon Labs and the Patent

One of the highlights was my time at **AT&T Shannon Research Labs**. (Yes, *that* Shannon — information theory.) I worked on problems that lived right at the boundary between classical bits and something deeper. Out of that work came a patent for multifactor risk analysis and the first voice and video and device authentication system. 

It felt like closing a loop: from Dad’s pencil on graph paper to a real U.S. patent.

### Retirement — and the Real Work Begins

Eventually I retired. Most people slow down. I finally sped up.

With no more day-job distractions, I sat down and started writing the code, the proofs, the documents that became the Quantum Logical Framework. QuCalc, RhoQuCalc, the 8-twist algebra, the ZFA closure — everything that had been simmering since I was six years old finally poured out.

Dad had passed away years earlier, but every commit message still ends with a quiet “thanks, Dad” in my head.

### Why I’m Sharing This Now

This repository isn’t just code or proofs. It’s the continuation of a conversation I started on my father’s knee — through school, trucks, college, marriage, pioneering computer networks, consulting, patents, and finally into retirement where the real work could begin.

If you’re reading this, you’re part of that conversation now.

So pull up a chair. Ask the dumb questions. Draw the twists. Break the rules the way nature does — with perfect bookkeeping.

The books have to balance.  
Everything else is just a story we tell while we figure out how.

And if you ever get stuck, remember what Dad always said:

“Start from zero. Stay honest. And never be afraid to look ridiculous.”

Welcome to the family business.
